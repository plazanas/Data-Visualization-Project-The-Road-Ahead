02 What actually decides it

One chart, four suspects.

EV share of new cars in 2024 across European countries. Keep scrolling and the horizontal axis changes to test different factors.

r —

First suspect: wealth

Richer countries do tend to adopt faster, the strongest single link we found (rank ρ ≈ —), but there are exceptions, Ireland and Luxembourg are rich, yet they lag.

r —

The intuition that fails: electricity price

Cheaper electricity does not mean more EVs, Hungary has Europe's cheapest power and a 7% share, Denmark pays far more and sits above 50%.

r —

The whole running-cost gap

Petrol and diesel cost savings compared to EV per 100 km are also weak correlations. Money matters, but on its own it doesn't decide.

r —

A policy lever you can measure: charging

Public charging point per 100k people track adoption better than anything else.

policy

The missing axis: policy

Split at the medians and two clusters appear that cost can't separate, policy-led adopters (small saving, high share) and cost-rich but stalled (big saving, low share). Purchase subsidies, EV tax and VAT breaks paired with high taxes on petrol/diesel cars (Norway) and company-car tax rules (Netherlands) are all policies that push for EV adaptation.

06 The acceleration

The leaders pulled away.

Battery-electric share of new cars, every country, since 2013. Adoption isn't drifting up, for the front-runners it went vertical.

BEV share of new cars: 2019 → 2024
Hover any line. Highlighted = the five fastest risers by 2024 share.
Five years, two different worlds.

Denmark went from 2% to 51%, Norway from 41% to 88%, Malta from 4% to 38%. Meanwhile Poland, Croatia and Slovakia barely left the floor, the gap isn't closing, it's widening.
